About Fishes

Fish are aquatic vertebrates that have lived on Earth for more than 500 million years, making them one of the oldest and most diverse groups of animals. They are found in nearly every type of water environment, from the deepest oceans to small mountain streams, and they play a vital role in maintaining the balance of aquatic ecosystems. Most fish breathe through gills, have fins for movement, and are covered in scales that protect their bodies, while their streamlined shapes help them swim efficiently. They are generally cold-blooded, meaning their body temperature changes with the water around them. Scientists classify fish into three main groups: jawless fish, cartilaginous fish such as sharks and rays, and bony fish, which make up the largest group and include familiar species like salmon, tuna, and goldfish. Beyond their biological importance, fish are also essential to humans as a major source of food, livelihood through fishing industries, and cultural symbols of prosperity and life in many traditions. Their incredible diversity, ranging from tiny species only a few millimeters long to giant whale sharks, shows how well they have adapted to different environments, making fish one of the most remarkable and valuable groups of animals on our planet.
